Every medical expert witness has been in this position.

You're six months past the review. You wrote a thorough report. You're confident in your conclusions. Then, in deposition, opposing counsel asks: "Doctor, can you show me exactly where in the medical records you found the basis for that opinion?"

You remember the document. You remember the passage. But you can't find it — not quickly, not precisely. You're scrolling through a 3,000-page PDF with no bookmarks and no links. The clock is ticking. The court reporter is waiting.

Your opinion isn't wrong. But right now, it's unverifiable. And in litigation, unverifiable is indistinguishable from wrong.

## The provenance gap

Provenance, in the context of expert work, means a traceable chain from every assertion in your report back to a specific location in a specific source document. Not "somewhere in the GP records." Not "I recall seeing it in the hospital notes." A precise, navigable link from conclusion to evidence.

Most expert witnesses don't have this. Not because they're careless, but because their tools don't support it.

The standard workflow looks something like this: review records in Adobe Acrobat, take notes in Word, build a chronology in Excel, write the report in another Word document. At no point in this process does any tool create a connection between what you wrote and where you found it. The analysis is structurally disconnected from the evidence.

The provenance chain was never built. It was always just memory.

## How provenance breaks down

The gap doesn't appear during the review itself. During the review, everything is fresh — you know exactly which file, which page, which paragraph. The problem surfaces later, when the work product has to stand on its own:

**At deposition.** You're asked to substantiate an assertion from your report. You wrote it three months ago. The corpus was 4,200 pages across 47 documents. You're reasonably certain it was in the discharge summary from St. Thomas', but you're not certain enough to say so under oath.

**Under peer review.** A second expert is asked to evaluate your reasoning. They receive your report but have no way to verify your citations against the original records without manually searching the entire corpus themselves.

**When the case evolves.** New records arrive. The chronology needs updating. But your original notes don't link to their sources, so you can't efficiently determine what you've already reviewed and what's new.

In each scenario, the problem is the same: the connection between your professional judgment and the evidence that supports it exists only in your head.

## What a defensible workflow actually looks like

A provenance-preserving workflow has three characteristics:

**Every annotation is anchored to its source.** When you highlight a passage, note a finding, or record a date for your chronology, that annotation is permanently linked to the exact page and location in the original document. Not to a file name. Not to a page number you typed manually. To the source itself — navigable in one click.

**The original documents are never modified.** Your annotations, notes, and analysis exist in a separate layer. The corpus remains intact. If anyone questions whether the records you reviewed are the same records produced in discovery, the answer is verifiable: the originals are untouched, with integrity confirmed by cryptographic hash.

**The report traces back to the evidence.** Every citation in your final report links to the specific highlight, on the specific page, in the specific document. A reader — whether that's opposing counsel, a judge, or a peer reviewer — can follow the chain from assertion to source without asking you to explain where you found it.

## The cost of the gap

The provenance gap doesn't always cause problems. Many cases settle. Many depositions don't probe this deeply. But when it does cause problems, they're significant:

- An opinion that can't be substantiated on the stand gets excluded or discredited.
- A report without traceable citations invites a Daubert challenge on methodology.
- An agency whose experts produce inconsistent, unverifiable work product develops a reputation — and not the kind that attracts top panelists.

The uncomfortable truth is that most expert witnesses are one aggressive cross-examination away from discovering that their workflow doesn't support the claims their reports make.
